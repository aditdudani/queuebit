QueueBit: A Hardware Dispatcher for Online Syndrome Routing in Surface-Code DecodingAbstractReal-time quantum error correction requires classical control logic that can route syndrome events to processing units without creating unsafe overlap between concurrently active work regions. This report presents QueueBit, a hardware dispatcher for online syndrome routing in surface-code decoding pipelines. QueueBit is not a full decoder. It is a control-plane component that receives syndrome coordinates, checks them against the active lock state, and either issues them to a worker or stalls until the hazard clears. The design is evaluated at two operating points, d=11 and d=23, using worker-latency and offered-load sweeps. The results show that QueueBit behaves predictably across both scales, with clear source-limited and saturated regimes. A naive baseline without hazard checking produces 138 unsafe concurrent pairs out of 439 concurrent pairs, confirming that collision-aware dispatch is functionally necessary. Post-route FPGA implementation on XC7Z020 meets the applied 100 MHz timing target at both d=11 and d=23. The d=23 results also show substantial blocked hazard activity at larger scale and heavier load, which makes d=23 the main platform for discussing operating limits.1. IntroductionSurface-code quantum error correction depends on fast classical processing. Syndrome bits must be collected, interpreted, and routed to decoder logic quickly enough that the correction pipeline does not fall behind the quantum system. Much of the literature focuses on the decoder itself, for example matching-based decoders, Union-Find decoders, and cluster-based methods. However, before a worker or decoder stage can process a syndrome, that syndrome must first be assigned safely.This report studies that assignment problem. If two workers are allowed to process nearby syndromes at the same time, they may operate on overlapping spatial regions. In a practical decoding pipeline this creates a coordination problem. QueueBit addresses this by placing a hardware dispatch layer in front of the workers. The dispatcher keeps track of active regions, tests whether a new syndrome would conflict with the current lock state, and either issues the syndrome immediately or stalls it until the hazard clears.The contribution of this work is therefore not a new decoder algorithm. It is a hardware dispatch mechanism for online, collision-aware syndrome routing. The report focuses on three questions:Is active hazard checking actually necessary?How does the dispatcher behave as worker latency and offered load change?Does the design remain implementable when scaled from a smaller d=11 case to a larger d=23 case?These questions are answered using a naive baseline, d=11 and d=23 sweeps, and post-route FPGA implementation results.2. BackgroundPractical quantum computers require continuous error correction to prevent physical noise from destroying logical quantum states. In surface codes [6], physical qubits are arranged in a 2D lattice, and parity measurements are repeatedly performed to detect errors. A change in consecutive parity measurements forms a "defect" or "syndrome" event [2].If syndrome data is not processed quickly enough, the decoder creates a backlog that grows exponentially with the depth of the computation, ultimately halting the system [3]. Real-time decoding prevents this backlog by processing syndrome events continuously as they are generated.However, routing these syndrome events to parallel decoding units creates spatial collision hazards. If two nearby syndromes are issued to different parallel workers simultaneously, their correction operations may overlap and interfere.3. Related WorkRecent hardware decoders handle collision avoidance through different architectural approaches:Barber et al. (2025) [1]: Reactive clustering. The match unit performs pairwise collision checks during cluster growth. This approach targets batch offline processing and handles collisions internally during the matching phase.QUEKUF (2025) [8]: Centralized controller. It orchestrates processing unit assignment with internal collision logic. The architecture is designed for stateful multi-round cluster processing on toric codes.Kasamura et al. (2025) [5]: Per-round batching. Sequential processing within individual measurement rounds ensures collision-free operation implicitly. This targets online surface code Union-Find decoding.Helios (2024) [7]: Distributed Union-Find decoder for surface codes. It assigns a processing unit to each vertex in the decoding graph, relying on a fully distributed network to manage operations.QueueBit targets a different point in the design space: stateless per-syndrome online dispatch for surface codes. Rather than checking clusters post-hoc (Barber), managing internal multi-round state (QUEKUF), or relying on per-round boundaries (Kasamura), QueueBit enforces dispatch-time spatial separation externally via a hardware matrix. This enables the use of modular, stateless workers—simple processing units that handle one syndrome at a time and release it, without maintaining complex cluster state. QueueBit is complementary to prior work by providing the external control plane that enables this simpler worker architecture.4. ScopeQueueBit is designed for surface-code workloads where syndrome events arrive over time and must be routed to a centralized worker pool. The key assumption is that each active worker occupies a local spatial neighborhood for a configurable number of cycles. During that time, issuing a nearby syndrome to another worker is unsafe and should be prevented.QueueBit's role is to ensure that whatever decoder follows receives a spatially safe stream of syndrome assignments. It sits between syndrome generation and downstream worker logic. It provides an online dispatch policy that can be placed in front of simple per-syndrome workers. The measurements presented here are dispatcher-level results demonstrating that QueueBit accepts, delays, and routes syndromes safely.5. ArchitectureQueueBit coordinates dispatch through a hardware pipeline connecting an input queue, an internal state machine, a spatial tracking matrix, and an active worker pool.Dispatcher Block Diagram:[Syndrome Stream] -> (Syndrome FIFO) -> (Dispatch FSM <-> Tracking Matrix) -> [4-Worker Pool]
5.1 Syndrome FIFOThe FIFO buffers incoming syndrome coordinates. It decouples the arrival stream from the dispatch logic and provides the next candidate syndrome to the FSM. The interface uses a valid/ready protocol. For both operating points, the depth of the FIFO is configured to FIFO_DEPTH=32.5.2 Tracking MatrixThe tracking matrix stores the currently locked spatial regions. Collision checking is performed against this matrix before a new syndrome is issued.The hardware uses a 3x3 Chebyshev neighborhood lock. When a syndrome is dispatched, the matrix synchronously sets a 3x3 region of grid cells centered on the syndrome coordinate to an active state. When the worker completes its task, it issues a release command, and the matrix clears that specific 3x3 region. The matrix supports combinatorial reads to allow the FSM to check for collisions in a single cycle.For the d=11 operating point, the matrix models a 21x23 grid (GRID_WIDTH=21, GRID_HEIGHT=23), utilizing 5-bit coordinate widths. The d=23 scale-up configuration uses a larger 47x47 grid (GRID_WIDTH=47, GRID_HEIGHT=47) requiring 6-bit coordinate widths.5.3 Dispatch FSMThe FSM coordinates the pipeline using five states: FSM_IDLE, FSM_FETCH, FSM_HAZARD_CHK, FSM_ISSUE, and FSM_STALL.It reads the next syndrome from the FIFO and evaluates it in FSM_HAZARD_CHK. If the 3x3 combinatorial read returns an overlap with an existing lock, it moves to FSM_STALL until a worker finishes and the hazard clears. Otherwise, it moves to FSM_ISSUE where the syndrome is dispatched to the first available unit in the 4-worker pool (NUM_WORKERS=4), and the corresponding matrix region is locked.6. Discussion8.1 Interpretation of d=23 Hazard DetectionsThe non-zero hazard-detection counts at d=23 are a key performance result. They should not be described as evidence of safety failures. Instead, they show that under larger scale, longer worker occupancy, and heavier offered load, the dispatcher encounters many candidate issues that conflict with the current lock state. QueueBit correctly blocks these issues. This establishes the operating envelope where the dispatcher becomes contention-limited.8.2 Baseline Saturation and Uniform Low-Load StallsAt low offered loads (inj=0.1), the source itself limits throughput, resulting in a large runtime drop when moving to inj=1.0 as the workload finishes faster.Furthermore, at inj=0.1 for d=23, the stall fractions are uniformly flat (~9.8% to 9.9%) regardless of K. At this low injection rate, syndromes arrive so infrequently that they rarely overlap in the temporal processing window. The stall fraction saturates at a baseline level driven by the spatial proximity of the syndrome data itself rather than temporal crowding.8.3 Scalability and Contention Pressure at d=23The d=23 scale-up exposes stronger contention effects, stronger sensitivity to worker occupancy, and measurable blocked hazard activity under load. This confirms d=23 as the more informative stress case for establishing where the dispatcher experiences bounding limits.9. Limitations and Future WorkQueueBit is evaluated as a dispatcher, not as a full decoder, and the current worker model is abstract. The synthesis results reflect the internal design logic under current constraints, rather than a full board-level interface study.Future work will focus on integrating QueueBit with a full reference Union-Find decoder, scaling the worker pool beyond 4 units, and executing physical board-level validation.10. ConclusionThis report presented QueueBit, a hardware dispatcher for online syndrome routing in surface-code decoding pipelines. The experimental results support four main conclusions:Scaling to d=23 remains implementable on XC7Z020 at the applied 100 MHz target, but the larger distance exposes substantial blocked hazard activity and contention under load.Hazard checking is strictly necessary. The naive baseline produces 138 spatial violations out of 439 concurrent pairs, confirming that routing requires active coordination.QueueBit shows clear source-limited and saturated regimes at both d=11 and d=23, adapting smoothly as offered load changes.Larger worker latency increases runtime and contention pressure, driving the system into a worker-limited operating envelope at high values of K.These results validate the feasibility of stateless per-syndrome online dispatch, providing a clean scale-up comparison and a practical hardware implementation story.References[1] Ben Barber et al.
